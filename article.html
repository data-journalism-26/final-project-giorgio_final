At the end of January 2026, the Cyclone Harry stumbled upon Southern Italy and the North African coast, causing intense damage across Sicily and Calabria, including a landslide in the town of Niscemi, where, almost miraculously, no one was killed. A related story, just few miles away, happened the same days during the storm: more than a thousand people have been missing or found dead in the Central Mediterranean, right after the passage of the Cyclon. Over the consequent weeks, few bodies of anonymuous migrants have washed up on the shores of Sicily and Calabia. They are only few of the many that departed from Tunisia between the 14 and 21 January 2026, as they were attempting crossing the Central Mediterranean Route to reach the Italian shores. The reported numbers are based on testimonies gathered in Tunisia by activists from Refugees in Libya and by the families of people who have not been heard from for days, and the data are consolidated by different independent organizations.

The Central Mediterranean Route is considered the most dangeous migratory route in the world by many organizations, including the International Organization for Migration, IOM (source). Data and methods. Surface wind, mean sea-level pressure and other ERA5 variables were retrieved from the Copernicus Climate Data Store for the period 12 January – 17 February 2026 (hourly, 0.25° grid, 45°N–28°N / 5°W–25°E). Shipwreck records are from UNITED for Intercultural Action's List of Refugee Deaths, filtered to those whose cause-of-death text mentions Cyclone Harry. The IOM Missing Migrants Project figures are obtained by filtering its Central Mediterranean route entries to the same window. Basemap from CartoDB · OpenStreetMap contributors, used under ODbL. All code, data, methodological note and replication package are at github.com/data-journalism-26/final-project-giorgio_final.
