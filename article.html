The Cyclone Harry on the Central Mediterranean migration route

In late January 2026, a Mediterranean cyclone swept across the Strait of Sicily as hundreds of people attempted the sea crossing from Tunisia. More than a thousand were reported dead or missing.

Giorgio Coppola  ·  19 May 2026

Opening video: Refugees in Libya, Instagram reel (2026)

Scroll

At the end of January 2026, Cyclone Harry swept across Southern Italy and the North African coast, causing intense damage across Sicily and Calabria, including a landslide in the town of Niscemi — where, almost miraculously, no one was killed. A related story, just few miles away, unfolded the same days on the water: more than a thousand people were reported missing or found dead in the Central Mediterranean, right after the passage of the storm. Over the following weeks, a handful of anonymous bodies washed ashore on the beaches of Sicily and Calabria.

They are only a few of the many who had departed from the Tunisian coast between the 14th and 21st of January 2026, attempting the Central Mediterranean Route to reach Italian shores. The reported figures are based on testimonies gathered in Tunisia by activists from Refugees in Libya and by the families of people who had not been heard from in days. The data were then consolidated by independent organizations including UNITED for Intercultural Action and the IOM's Missing Migrants Project.

The Central Mediterranean Route is considered the most dangerous migratory sea route in the world. Every year, hundreds of people die or disappear trying to cross roughly 300 kilometres of open sea. In this case, only one person survived. The cyclone was not the only cause — overloaded rubber dinghies, the absence of rescue vessels, the lack of legal pathways — but it was the trigger that turned a dangerous journey into a mass casualty event.

That one survivor was Ramadan Konte, a citizen of Sierra Leone. According to his testimony, reported by the NGO Mediterranea, he had departed from Sfax on one of the many boats leaving the Tunisian coast in those weeks, carrying around fifty people of different nationalities. The boat capsized during the storm. Konte survived for more than 24 hours in the open sea before being spotted by the merchant ship Star, captained by Ahmed Omar Shafik, east of Tunisia and south of Malta. As he was pulled aboard, bodies were visible floating in the water around him. From that single vessel he lost his brother, his brother's wife, his nephew, and at least 47 others. The other boats that departed Tunisia in those same days are presumed lost — their passengers among the more than a thousand reported dead or missing.

12 January 2026

Departures begin

In the weeks preceding the storm, hundreds of people prepared to leave Tunisia in small rubber dinghies and wooden boats. The Central Mediterranean Route — roughly 300 kilometres between the Tunisian and Libyan coasts and the Italian island of Lampedusa — is the world's most-documented deadly migration corridor.

22 January 2026 — Storm peak

The cyclone reaches its peak

On 22 January, Cyclone Harry intensified over the Strait of Sicily. Sustained winds exceeded 80 km/h and wave heights rose sharply across the crossing zone. Multiple boats in the water at the time capsized or were swamped.

Of the hundreds of people who had departed from Tunisia in the days prior, only a single person was pulled from the sea alive — rescued after being spotted by a ship, clinging to a lifebuoy. The photograph at the top of this page shows that moment.

The satellite view above shows the cyclone from space: the coldest cloud tops (white and magenta) mark the deepest convection at the storm's centre. Toggle to the wind & pressure view to see the same period through ERA5 meteorological data.

February 2026 — Calabria

Bodies wash ashore

In the weeks after the storm, the sea returned some of what it had taken. Bodies began washing up on the beaches of Lampedusa, Sicily, and the coast of Calabria — the toe of the Italian boot. The visualization zooms into the Calabrian coast as circles accumulate on shore.

Each body recovered represented a family waiting for news. Activists and forensic teams worked to identify the deceased, many of whom had no documentation. Most will remain unnamed.

17 February 2026 — Final count

Over a thousand dead or missing

By mid-February, UNITED for Intercultural Action had recorded 20 incidents directly linked to Cyclone Harry, accounting for 1,109 deaths. The IOM's Missing Migrants Project — which tracks a broader set of events including subsequent body recoveries along Italian and Libyan coasts — registered 25 events in the same window, totalling 499 dead or missing.

The real toll is almost certainly higher. Many boats never make it to a shore that could report them missing. Many bodies are never recovered.

The discrepancy between UNITED and IOM reflects different methodologies, not different events. See the methodology note below.

Data and methodology

1. Data sources

This piece is built from five datasets: two meteorological / satellite raster products, two incident lists of recorded migrant deaths, and one set of administrative-zone polygons. Every dataset used in the article is listed below.

  • ERA5 hourly reanalysis — 10 m wind, mean sea-level pressure and significant wave height over the Mediterranean, 0.25° grid. Source: Copernicus Climate Data Store, retrieved through the cdsapi client (free CDS account required).
  • Meteosat MSG SEVIRI IR_108 — 10.8 µm cloud-top brightness temperature, cropped to the Mediterranean at 3-hourly cadence. Source: EUMETSAT Data Store, retrieved through the eumdac client (HRSEVIRI usage license required).
  • UNITED for Intercultural Action — List of Refugee Deaths — Network-compiled list of refugee and migrant deaths at Europe's borders since 1993. Filtered to the 20 entries whose cause-of-death description explicitly names Cyclone Harry (1,109 deaths). The raw data is not redistributed in this repository; the filtered CSV is available on request. Source: unitedagainstrefugeedeaths.eu.
  • IOM Missing Migrants Project — Incident-level deaths and disappearances of migrants globally, May 2026 snapshot. Filtered to the Central Mediterranean route between 14 January and 17 February 2026 (25 events, 499 dead or missing). The raw data is not redistributed in this repository; the filtered CSV is available on request. Source: missingmigrants.iom.int.
  • IMO Search-and-Rescue zone polygons — Boundary polygons for the Italian, Maltese, Tunisian and Libyan SAR zones, parsed from the official GML files at the IMO GISIS Global SAR Plan portal (registration required).
  • CartoDB Voyager (no labels) — Basemap tiles. © OpenStreetMap contributors, used under ODbL.

2. The meteorological animation

The first animation shows how Cyclone Harry developed and moved across the Central Mediterranean between 12 January and 17 February 2026. The colour shading represents wind speed — from calm (white) through moderate (green/yellow) to intense (orange/red) — while the contour lines trace atmospheric pressure and their concentric pattern marks the cyclone's centre. Both variables come from ERA5, a global atmospheric reanalysis produced by the Copernicus Climate Change Service. ERA5 is not a satellite measurement: it is a computational reconstruction of the state of the atmosphere, combining observational data from many sources with a numerical weather model to produce a consistent gridded dataset going back to 1940. The animation samples one frame every three hours.

3. The infrared satellite animation

The second animation shows the same period from a different angle: what the Meteosat satellite saw looking down at the storm. The data come from the MSG SEVIRI instrument on board Meteosat, operated by EUMETSAT, which measures the temperature of cloud tops in the infrared. Cold cloud tops (shown in white and magenta) indicate tall, active convection — the engine of the storm. Warm values (grey) indicate low cloud or the sea surface. Unlike ERA5, this is a direct satellite observation, not a model output. The satellite takes a full-disk image of Earth every 15 minutes; the animation uses one frame every three hours, matching the ERA5 cadence. Both animations zoom progressively into the coast of Calabria from 1 February onwards, where bodies washed ashore in the weeks following the storm.

4. The incident data: UNITED for Intercultural Action

UNITED for Intercultural Action has maintained its List of Refugee Deaths since 1993, documenting individual cases of people who have died at or because of the borders of Europe. The list is compiled from reports by a network of roughly 550 organisations across 48 countries, supplemented by journalists, researchers, and local experts. Each entry is individually verified and includes the date, location, number of deaths, and a brief description of the cause. For this article, the dataset was filtered to the 20 entries whose cause-of-death description explicitly names Cyclone Harry, yielding 1,109 recorded deaths. This filter is deliberate but limiting: it counts only events where the connection to the storm was explicitly documented at the time, and will miss cases where the link was never established or recorded.

5. The incident data: IOM Missing Migrants Project

The IOM Missing Migrants Project, run by the International Organization for Migration, tracks deaths and disappearances of migrants globally. It draws on a wider set of sources than UNITED — coast guard reports, IOM field offices, media, NGOs — and aims to record every incident in a given migration corridor within a given time window, regardless of whether a specific cause (such as a storm) has been attributed. For this article, the dataset (May 2026 snapshot) was filtered to the Central Mediterranean route between 14 January and 17 February 2026, producing 25 events totalling 499 dead or missing. This figure includes body recoveries that occurred days or weeks after the storm, along the coasts of Italy and Libya. The two datasets are not directly comparable and should not be summed: they answer different questions and use different definitions of what counts as an event.

6. The interactive map

Each circle on the map represents one recorded event in the relevant dataset. The size of the circle scales with the number of dead or missing reported for that event, so large circles indicate incidents with many casualties and small circles indicate one or a few. The map uses a proportional symbol approach: circles are not sized to match each other exactly, but to give a visual sense of the relative scale of each event. The Search and Rescue zone boundaries shown in grey indicate which country's coast guard has nominal responsibility for rescue operations in each area; they are provided as geographic context, not as a judgment about responsibility.
